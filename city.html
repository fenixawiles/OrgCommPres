City & Regional Impact

How the Merger Transforms San Antonio and the Broader South Texas Region

A Transformational Value Proposition for San Antonio

The UT System’s Integration FAQ describes the merger’s “value proposition for San Antonio” as adding immeasurable value by uniting the complementary strengths of UTSA and UT Health San Antonio. Together, the institutions expand academic programs, accelerate research in health sciences and engineering, increase health-care delivery capacity, and enhance innovation and economic development efforts throughout the metropolitan region.

The unification is framed as a once-in-a-generation opportunity to elevate San Antonio’s standing as a global leader in academia, health care, and research. This integrated structure positions the city to compete more aggressively for national grants, research centers, philanthropic investment, and industry partnerships.

Strengthening San Antonio’s Largest Industry

UT San Antonio plays a central role in the city’s $44.1 billion healthcare and biosciences sector, the largest industry in the metropolitan economy. UT Health San Antonio has long been a major contributor to this sector; as noted in Sombrilla Magazine, UT Health’s research has generated medications, therapies, and clinical treatments that impact both local and global populations.

Post-merger, with the unified institution reporting $7 billion in annual economic impact and nearly half a billion dollars in annual research expenditures, San Antonio gains a single, consolidated anchor for the entire life-sciences, biotech, and clinical research ecosystem.

This consolidation places San Antonio among the nation’s emerging hubs for health innovation, medical technology, and biotechnology commercialization.

Expanded Clinical Presence and Regional Health Services

UT Health San Antonio’s clinical enterprise was already substantial prior to the merger, consisting of:

  • 60 patient care sites
  • A 32-county service region
  • More than 2.5 million patient visits annually
  • Over 500 active clinical trials
  • The opening of the $471 million Multispecialty & Research Hospital in 2024

The UT System FAQ highlights that the merger will “amplify the clinical enterprise”, enabling deeper collaboration between UT Health physicians, the Multispecialty and Research Hospital, UTSA researchers, and the broader South Texas Medical Center—which serves a 38-county population.

These developments produce a more unified, coherent, and coordinated health-care infrastructure for San Antonio and the surrounding region, improving access to advanced clinical care, specialized treatments, and cutting-edge trials.

Elevating San Antonio’s National Profile

Regents Chairman Kevin Eltife stated that great cities and great universities “make each other better,” framing the merger as a major civic moment for San Antonio’s growth trajectory. By consolidating talent, size, research capacity, and clinical reach, the city is positioned to become a national and global leader in:

  • Biomedical research
  • Clinical health innovation
  • AI-driven medicine
  • Biotechnology and life sciences
  • STEM education and workforce development

Mission Magazine describes the merger as “the catalyst that will supercharge our region as a national leader in learning, innovation, health and economic vigor.”

This transformation aligns with San Antonio’s rapid population growth and its strategic position as the gateway to a multi-million-person South Texas region.

Key Takeaways for San Antonio

Based on official UT System, UTSA, and UT Health sources, the merger ensures that:

  • San Antonio solidifies its identity as a major research and medical city, not just a military or tourism economy.
  • The city’s $44.1B healthcare sector gains a unified academic and clinical anchor.
  • Local residents gain expanded access to advanced clinical care through:
    • the Multispecialty & Research Hospital,
    • 60+ patient care sites,
    • a large and expanding clinical trial portfolio.
  • The region’s workforce pipelines in medicine, dentistry, nursing, public health, engineering, and biomedical sciences grow stronger and more coordinated.
